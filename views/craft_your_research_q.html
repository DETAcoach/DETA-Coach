Craft your research question

Your research question will drive the rest of your study. A good question has four building blocks, which we call the "A, B, C, D's". Click on sections A through D below to see examples, read a description, and build your own question, piece by piece.

The blue print: Does A do B among C compared to D?

Example: Does eZumi Learning increase student achievement on the benchmark assessment for mathamong 5th grade English Learners compared to similar students with no access to eZumi Learning?

Answer the 4 questions below to see an automatically generated research question.

NOTE: Make sure you do not include any personally identifiable information, such as names (teachers or students) or descriptors that would reveal who your participants are.

Things to Consider

What group of individuals are you trying to affect? For example, students may be using the technology to increase their own achievement or teachers could be using the technology to increase their students' achievement. In both cases, the answer to Part C would be: students.

Example

  • 6th grade students
  • 5th grade English learners
  • 3rd through 5th grade teachers

Things to Consider

It is important to be clear about whether you are comparing users to a group with no access to an educational technology, or with access to a different educational technology. As shown above, this will determine how you interpret your findings later on and what kinds of decisions this data will empower you to make.

Example

  • Similar 5th grade English Learners with no access to eZumi Learning
  • Similar 5th grade English Learners that use IXL Math instead of eZumi Learning
