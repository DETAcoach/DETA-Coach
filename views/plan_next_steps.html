The Coach will help you think about how the answer to your research question will inform the decisions you make regarding your technology. To complete this tool you will need to consider:

  • What would success look like or what is the minimum meaningful effect (MME)? The minimum meaningful effect is the minimum amount of change you would need to see between your treatment and comparison groups in order to conclude the technology is having the intended effect and is preferable to the alternative. The MME will be different for different situations. For example, you may want a large MME for an app that requires a big investment, but be okay with a small change if the app is free. The MME should make sense given your outcome measure’s normal variability.

  • How much risk are you willing to take or what is your certainty threshold? The certainty threshold is the amount of uncertainty you are willing to accept when making a decision or a recommendation based on your evaluation. You might want a higher level of certainty, and therefore a higher probability, if your results will affect a higher stakes decision, such as implementing a district-wide curriculum. You might be comfortable with a lower probability for a lower stakes decision.

    You make these types of decisions all the time, for example, when you decide whether or not to take an umbrella after looking at the day’s weather forecast. Some people will take an umbrella if the probability of rain is 50%, others might only take an umbrella if an 80% chance is forecasted. That is, different people have different thresholds for how much risk they are willing to take. And you may have a different threshold for different kinds of decision.

Things to Consider

The cost of a technology could simply be the cost per user. It could also be the savings realized by replacing one technology with another less expensive technology. Cost can also be measured in something other than dollars, such as teacher preparation time saved.

Example

  • It costs about $20 per student to use the educational technology.
  • It saves about $1,000 per classroom to use the educational technology.
  • We're not sure. We believe using this tool will save teachers 20 minutes per day on prep time.

Things to Consider

  • Remember that the Coach will compare average outcomes for the treatment group to average outcomes for the comparison group. This difference is what will be compared to the MME.
  • To choose the MME, consider the range of values for that outcome. If it’s unusual to see differences of 10 points between groups of students, then 10 might be too high.
  • Make sure the number you select here matches the magnitude of the outcomes in your data. For example, if your units are percent, you could use values between 0 and 1 or between 0 and 100. If you use values between 0 and 1 in your dataset you should put a value between 0 and 1 here.

Things to Consider

  • The default setting is 75%, but you should consider whether this is appropriate for your decision.
  • If you choose a probability that is too high, for example 100, the Coach will likely conclude that there is not enough evidence.
  • The Coach will calculate this probability based on the data you provide. You should only believe these findings to the extent you believe you have a good comparison group in the data.

D. What will you do if …

… the data suggests that the treatment group does better than the comparison group.

… the data suggests that the treatment group does worse than the comparison group.

… the data suggests that the two groups are equivalent.

… the results are inconclusive (i.e. none of the bars exceed the threshold).

Things to Consider

The Coach will use your MME to divide the range of possible outcomes into three regions:

  • the treatment group does better than the comparison group by an amount at least as large as the MME
  • the two groups are equivalent
  • the treatment group does worse than the comparison group by an amount at least as large as the MME
To help you answer your research question, the Coach will calculate the propability of each of these three possibilities being the actual outcome and compare those propabilities to your certainty threshold.
