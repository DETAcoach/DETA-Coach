Evaluating your technology will require you to have two groups, a treatment group and a comparison group. The individuals in each group and the engagement they have with a technology will vary based on your context and the question you would like to answer. This tool will help you identify the best approach for creating these two groups.

If you’re already using the technology, the Coach will check that you meet the requirements to conduct a backward-looking evaluation. If you're piloting a new technology or expanding a current one to new users, the Coach will recommend one of several forward-looking evaluations.

If you want to get a better understanding of how your design and your research question interact, please read Limitations on the Type of Question You Can Answer.

 

Can you create two similar groups? For example, one of <%= defs.Basics_Users %> using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> and one of <%= defs.Basics_Users %> who have yet to use <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>, or two groups using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> in different ways.

Will you conduct this evaluation with current users of <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>, or do you plan to conduct it with new users (as part of either an expansion or new implementation of <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>)?

Are all <%= defs.Basics_Users %> currently using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>?

Can you offer <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> to just a portion of new users, leaving you with a similar group of <%= defs.Basics_Users %> who have never used <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>?

Can you implement <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> in two distinct ways and create groups based on how users are engaging with <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>? For example, can you vary how often and for how long <%= defs.Basics_Users %> use <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>?

Do you have usage data that shows that there are <%= defs.Basics_Users %> who are “high users” and <%= defs.Basics_Users %> who are “low users”?

Things to Consider

If you are targeting a specific group of <%= defs.Basics_Users %> (for example English learners or Title 1 schools) answer this question only for those <%= defs.Basics_Users %>.

The more similar the two groups are at the start of the evaluation, the more confident you can be at the end of the evaluation that the measured differences found can be attributed to using the technology.

The Coach does not recommend comparing volunteers to non-volunteers because those that volunteer are inherently different from those that do not. If you must ask for volunteers, the Coach recommends selecting users and non-users from your pool of volunteers.

Sometimes using a technology in different ways can lead to different results. You may want to understand how the amount of time, or the number of days that the technology is used influences outcomes. For example, do students who use the technology for 120 minutes per week have better outcomes than students who only use it for 60 minutes per week? Or, do students who use the technology for 120 minutes over three days perform better than students who use the technology for 120 minutes over two days? You might also want to know if there’s a difference between using the technology during class time compared to using it at home, or whether unlocking certain features within the technology makes a difference. These are all implementation questions that you can answer using the Coach. However, it requires you to make sure your two groups each implement the technology in a specific way.

Your users may decide to engage with the technology in different ways. Some users may use the technology more, while others use it less. If you have access to usage data you can determine whether this is the case for your technology by looking at the number of logins or amount of time each user has used the technology. Then by setting a threshold that defines low and high usage you can categorize users into two groups. These two groups can be compared to provide suggestive evidence about how using the technology more influences outcomes.

The method you choose, will affect how confident you can be that your technology causes the outcomes you observe and how your findings will apply to a broader population of <%= defs.Basics_Users %>.

<% var checked = ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "random" ) ? "checked" : ""; var selected = ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "random" ) ? "selected" : ""; %>
/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

Random assignment is similar to putting the names of your <%= defs.Basics_Users %> in a hat and randomly picking some of the names. If you put in 10 names and select 5, those you pick would get to pilot (or try) the technology, and those not selected would continue with your existing program or curriculum. In this scenario, each individual would have a 50% chance of being chosen. The Coach can conduct this process for you using a digital hat and show you the similarities between the groups. This process can also work with teachers, classes, or even schools.

Advantages

  • Resulting treatment and comparison groups will, on average, be similar in both observed (for example, gender) and unobserved (for example, motivation) characteristics, giving you the most confidence that any differences in outcomes between the groups are due to using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>.
  • A fair way to decide who gets to use a technology first, because everyone has the same chance of being chosen.
  • Findings are relevant for the overall population you are working with (that is, everyone in the hat).

Disadvantage

  • Some <%= defs.Basics_Users %> get to use the technology earlier than others, which might be problematic for some stakeholders.
<% var checked = ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "cutoff" ) ? "checked" : ""; var selected = ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "cutoff" ) ? "selected" : ""; %>
/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

Using a cutoff means selecting who uses the technology based on where <%= defs.Basics_Users %> rank in relation to an existing threshold. For example, students just below a “low score” threshold are assigned to use <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>. We assume that students close to a cutoff are very similar, therefore, you can compare those just below the cutoff to those just above it.

Advantage

  • It allows for comparison of similar users without using random assignment.

Disadvantages

  • Findings apply only to <%= defs.Basics_Users %> near the cutoff, not to the whole population.
  • Might be less fair than random assignment. With this method, scoring 1 point below the cutoff gives you a 100% chance of using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>, whereas scoring 1 point above the cutoff means you have a 0% chance of using <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %>, even though both groups of <%= defs.Basics_Users %> might equally in need of it.
  • Not currently supported by the RCE Coach. Contact us if you’d like to discuss testing this new method.
<% var checked= ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "other" ) ? "checked" : ""; var selected = ( eval.probAppr.Appr_How_Choose == "other" ) ? "selected" : ""; %>
/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

Assignment to the treatment and comparison groups takes place in a nonrandom manner based on specific criteria. For example, the classrooms assigned to the pilot or treatment group are those with access to computers or the schools that will pilot the technology are chosen based on a competition or application process.

Advantages

  • Might align better with how you’ve assigned technology use in the past.
  • Might be logistically easier, particularly if you’re selecting based on who has easier access to computers or the Internet.

Disadvantage

  • Might not be considered fair to stakeholders. The group that meets the criteria might already have advantages over other <%= defs.Basics_Users %>.
  • It might not align with need. Those who need <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> more or would gain more from using it might be less likely to receive access than if you used random assignment.
  • It’s not always possible to create a good comparison group. If the Coach fails to create similar groups of users and non-users, you will not be able to determine whether the educational technology is moving the needle. It’s possible that groups appear similar, but unobserved differences, not included in your data, are driving outcomes.

Note: Across all of these methods, you do not have to create or compare groups at the individual level. If there are logistical barriers to working with individuals, you can create groups at higher levels, such as classrooms or schools. However, at higher levels you will need more study participants in order to be confident in your results.

Things to Consider

It might be more feasible to randomly assign <%= defs.Basics_Users %> by classroom or by school. However, your ability to learn whether <%= defs.Basics_Tech_Name %> is effective will be stronger if you can choose at the individual level.

Bigger is better. With only a few <%= defs.Basics_Users %> in your treatment and comparison groups, your results are more likely to be inconclusive. Larger samples enable you to detect smaller effects.
