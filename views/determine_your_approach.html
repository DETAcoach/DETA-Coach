Determine Your Approach

In any evaluation of effectiveness you will need to compare those using the technology (the intervention group) to a similar group not using the technology (the comparison group). The goal of this tool is to identify the best approach for creating these two groups.

If you're already using the technology, we'll consider options for a backward-looking evaluation. If you're allowing new users access to a technology you've already implemented or are not yet using, we can consider options for implementing or piloting the technology in a way that allows you to determine if it or works.

How will you determine which <%= Basics_Users %> get access to <%= Basics_Tech_Name %> and which <%= Basics_Users %> do not?

The method you choose below, will impact how confident you can be that your technology is causing the outcomes you observe and how applicable your findings are to a broader population of <%= Basics_Users %>.

Note: Across all of these methods, you do not need to create or compare groups at the individual level. If there are logistical barriers to working with individuals, you can create groups at higher levels, such as classrooms or schools. However, at higher levels you will need more study participants in order to be confident in your results

Do not compare volunteers to non-volunteers, because those that volunteer are inherently different from those that do not. If you must ask for volunteers, you should select users and non-users from your pool of volunteers.

/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

You can think of random assignment as putting the names of your <%= Basics_Users %> in a hat and randomly picking out some of the names. If you put in 10 names and selected 5, those you picked out would get to pilot (or try) the technology, while those whose names weren’t selected would continue with your existing program or curriculum. In this scenario, each individual would have a 50% chance of being chosen. The Coach can conduct this process for you using a digital hat and show you the similarities between the groups.

Advantages:

  • Recommended method. Resulting treatment and comparison groups will, on average, be similar in both observable and unobservable characteristics, giving you the most confidence that any differences in outcomes between the groups are due to using <%= Basics_Tech_Name %>.
  • A fair way to decide who gets to use a technology first, because everyone has the same chance of being chosen.
  • Allows for findings that are relevant for the overall population you are working with (i.e. everyone in the hat).

Disadvantages:

  • Some <%= Basics_Users %> get to use the technology earlier than others, which may be problematic for some stakeholders.

/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

Using a cutoff means selecting who uses the technology based on where <%= Basics_Users %> fall in relation to an existing threshold. For example, students just below the “low score” threshold are assigned to use <%= Basics_Tech_Name %>. We assume that students right around a cutoff are very similar. Therefore, you can compare those just below the cutoff to those just above it.

Advantages:

  • Allows for the mechanism of assignment to be based more on a specific measure than on chance.

Disadvantages:

  • Findings only apply to <%= Basics_Users %> near the cutoff, not to the whole population.
  • May be less fair than random assignment. With this method, scoring 1 point below the cutoff gives you a 100% chance of using <%= Basics_Tech_Name %>, while scoring 1 point above the cutoff means you have a 0% chance of using <%= Basics_Tech_Name %>, even though both groups of <%= Basics_Users %> may be equally in need of it.
  • Not currently supported by the RCE Coach, but will be coming soon! Contact us if you’d like to discuss testing this new method.

/> Description, advantages, & disadvantages

Assignment to the treatment and control groups is done in a non-random way based on specific criteria. For example, the classrooms assigned to the pilot or treatment group are those with access to computers, or the schools that will pilot the technology are chosen based on a competition or application process.

Advantages:

  • May align better with how you’ve assigned technology use in the past.
  • May be logistically easier, particularly if you are selecting based on who has easier access to computers or the Internet.

Disadvantages:

  • May not be considered fair to stakeholders. The group that meets the criteria may already have advantages over other <%= Basics_Users %>.
  • May not align with need. Those who need <%= Basics_Tech_Name %> more or would gain more from using it may be less likely to be given access than if random assignment was used.
  • It’s not always possible to create a good comparison group. If the Coach fails to create similar groups of users and non-users, you will not be able to determine if the educational technology is moving the needle. It’s possible that groups appear similar, but unobserved differences, not included in the your data, are driving outcomes.

Based on your answers, the Coach will guide you through a randomized pilot.

The Coach will use the Random Assignment tool to select <%= Basics_Users %> to be in your treatment and comparison groups and use the pretest and background characteristics data you provide to check that the groups are balanced or equivalent.

Since you will use a cutoff to determine who will use the technology, a Regression Discontinuity design (RD) is the suggested approach.

The Coach does not yet include tools for RD, but if you're interested in pursuing an evaluation please contact us at EdTechRCE@mathematica-mpr.com and you may be able to use beta versions of tools we are creating to handle this type of design.
