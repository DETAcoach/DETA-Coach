Who Used Your Technology and How?

Usage data (also called system or log data) is used to find out who is using education technology and how. This information can shed light on why a product is or isn’t having the desired effect. Download our in-depth guide for step-by-step instructions of making the most of your usage data.

Why you should use usage data

Usage data can help you answer several questions about your educational technology. If you’ve already implemented <%= Basics_Tech_Name %> you can examine how users are currently interacting with it before you even begin your evaluation. If you are implementing <%= Basics_Tech_Name %> with new users, referring to usage data throughout your evaluation will help you make sure the technology is being implemented as expected, making it possible for you to correct any issues as they arise.

Is the technology being used as expected?

If usage is very low, the technology may be unlikely to have an impact, and you may want to explore possible factors explaining patterns of use.

How is the technology being used?

How do users engage with the product (how much do they use the technology, patterns of usage over time, how they score on assessments embedded within the technology)? If you see patterns of low usage at specific points in time or within specific units of content, this may help identify factors that promote or prevent usage. Detailed information about usage can also help you understand your study’s main findings about the impact of the technology.

How to obtain usage data

Some products enable you to download usage data reports directly from their websites. For other products, or if the report isn’t what you need, you may need to contact the vendor.

Examples of data you might want to get:

Usage by individual

  • Student unique ID
  • Class / instructor ID
  • Duration of usage
  • Number of sessions
  • Average score on assessments

Usage by course topic

  • Topic(s) covered in each module
  • Attempted modules (which modules a student started)
  • Completed modules
  • Scores on assessments for each module

Usage over time

  • Duration of use by month
  • Number of sessions by month
  • Modules completed by month

Tips for getting useful usage data:

  • Think about whether you want data for individual users, or grouped by class, teacher or school.
  • If you are evaluating impact on students, make sure usage data include unique student IDs that can be matched with administrative records.
  • Ask for the report to have one row per student (as opposed to multiple rows per student).
  • Ask for formats that allow data to be manipulated, such as Microsoft Excel or CSV files, not PDFs.

Things to Consider

Make sure your data does not reveal any personally identifiable information (i.e. information that would identify specific individuals) where it is not necessary.

Some things to look for:

  • Do some students or classes have little or no usage?

  • How does actual usage compare to what the vendor says should happen?

    If the vendor says students should spend at least 90 minutes per week using the education technology, check to see how actual average weekly usage compares to this target.

  • Are there signs that students are “gaming the system”?

    If students make repeated attempts at assessments without spending time in instructional content, they may be trying to click their way through rather than learning the material.
