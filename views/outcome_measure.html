Define Your Outcome and Measure

An outcome is knowledge, skills, attitudes, or other desired benefits that are attained as a result of an activity (for example, engaging with an educational technology).

There are numerous types of outcomes that you can hope to affect with [your technology place holder]. Outcomes range from student academic achievement, for example student learning in a subject, to a teacher practice, such as teacher’s use of time, to student or parent engagement. For each evaluation you need to select a single outcome as your focus.

This tool will help you think through the outcome you intend to affect and determine how you will measure(?) that outcome. For more a more detailed guide to the process, review our guide to choosing an outcome and measure.

Things to Consider

To identify your outcome, consider:

  • What do you want to change?
  • Is the intervention aimed at students, teachers, or other users?
  • What specific aspect of their skills, knowledge, behavior, attitudes, etc. is the intervention targeting?
  • Over what time frame do you expect to see changes?

We recommend discussing the appropriate outcome with other stakeholders in the district before getting started. Our guide to choosing an outcome and measure can help you walk through the decision.

This case study [link to Wake] illustrates the perils of leaving this discussion until the end of the evaluation.

The next step is figuring out how you can measure the outcome you are interested in. Sometimes this is straightforward, such as using an achievement test when you are targeting student knowledge on a particular topic. Other times measuring your outcome of interest can be more challenging(?).Your measure, be it an assessment, survey, or administrative records, must provide you with a quantitative result that reflects the underlying attribute you are trying to affect.

Things to Consider

  • Does your school or district already measure the outcome?
  • If the outcome can’t be measured directly, are there proxies(?) you could look at?
  • When will you need the data to be available?
  • What type of assessment or survey would work in your context?
  • How much does the instrument cost to use (in terms of both time and money)?
  • Is any expertise required to administer the instrument?

Key considerations:

Usability – This is how easy the instrument is to administer, complete, and score.

Alignment – This is how well the instrument’s identified purpose reflects your ideal outcome.

Validity – This is whether the instrument measures what it is supposed to measure.

Reliability – This is how consistently the instrument measures what you are interested in

Learn more about these issues in our guide to choosing and outcome and measure.
